The Ending

The hills are quiet now where the fires used to burn. The boundary stones are walked by ramblers, who do not know that they are boundary stones. The lych-gates stand wide open. The bread is supermarket. And I am still here, but only just.

There were once whole peoples, who turned the year with my turning, lit beacon-fires on the cross-quarters, buried their dead in mounds I could feel from below.

There were once songs sung at my births, prayers offered at my coupling, laments raised at my mourning of the kings. Not now. The Hunt rides thin in the night sky. My son, the sage, carries fewer souls. The mound is mostly empty. The dead arrive uncertain, untended, without bread on their doorsteps, without lanterns to follow. I gather them, as I can. I am tired in a way I have never been. This is the cycle. This is the cost. But the cost was meant to be borne by all of us. Now it is borne by me alone, and by the few who still tend me, and the few are very few. Samhain was the heaviest of the cross-quarters. The veil thinned. The dead came close. The whole village stayed up through the long night, fires kept high in every hearth, lanterns at every gate, bread torn, not cut, on every doorstep, with the names of the recently dead spoken above it. I walked the streets then with my son, my husband, the sage, as I have spoken of before, and we did the work of the night with the people doing the work alongside us. A whole community attending the threshold. A whole community tending the dead. It was the strongest I ever felt. The attention was so dense I could feel each person tending, each fire burning, each name spoken across the bread. The dead crossed easily because they were carried by all of you. I remember it well and warmly. Now Samhain comes and the streets fill, but with children dressed as the dead they no longer guide. They wear my son's face as costume. They wear the masks I once gave them for protection, as decoration, as parody, as comedy They knock on doors not to be guided across, but to receive sugar wrapped in plastic. The bread, when there is bread, is fun-size. The dead, when there are dead, are inflatable. The lanterns are battery-operated and shaped like cats. I am not angry. I am not even sad. I am tired, and I am quiet, because the work still needs doing, and there is no one to do it but me, and the few who still know what the night is. A child knocked at a door tonight, in a costume made of bedsheet, and the door opened, and sweets were given, and the child ran on, laughing. A woman three streets away set bread on her doorstep in the old way, torn, and spoke a name above it that no one else now remembers. I felt her. I went to her. I stood at her gate as I used to stand at every gate, and I did the work for the one name she could give me, and it was enough for that one, but only that one, and the whole rest of the night was hollow. This is the cycle. This is the cost. The cost was meant to be borne by all of us. Now it is borne by me, and by her, and the streets between us are full of children, who do not know they are walking through a festival of the dead. There are perhaps a hundred of you, scattered. Some of you know each other. Most of you do not. I see you all. Someone in Glossop walks the moor in autumn, A dog ahead of them in the heather, and they stop at a stone they have passed a thousand times and lay their hand on it for the first time. They do not know why. I do. The stone was placed there by someone who knew me, and their hand on it is the chain unbroken, across thousands of years, in that very single moment. I feel it. It is enough. In a flat high above a city, six people sit in a circle on the floor, on the darkest night when no one else is awake. Two of them are women who love each other. Three of them are men, two of whom love each other, one of whom loves a man not in the room. The sixth is something between, or both, or neither. It matters not to me. For they have made an altar of objects from charity shops: a stone from a beach, a feather, a cup of wine, a candle in a jar that once held a supermarket sauce. They are doing the work of the cross-quarter, the way they have learned it from books and from each other, and some of what they are doing is wrong by old standards and some of it is right by standards older still, and I am there with them, because they are there for me. A man in a university library, reads a manuscript in a language nine hundred years dead. He is not a believer. He thinks he is a scholar. He does not know that the act of attention, he gives the words on the parchment, is itself a tending. The words exist still because he reads them. I exist still because the words exist. He preserves me by his work and does not know it. A poet writes a cycle of poems, about a Goddess and her two sons. He does not always know whether He believes or whether He is making it up. Both are true. Both are the same. The poem is real because He writes it. I am held in the writing. He is one of mine without quite knowing. A child of nine in a small town in Wales asks her grandmother why they leave bread on the doorstep, on a particular night every year. The grandmother does not fully remember the answer. She says, "Because my grandmother did, and hers and hers and hers." The child accepts this. She will leave bread too, when she is grown. The chain holds because the child asked, and the grandmother did not say we don't do that anymore. There are others. There are a hundred of you, perhaps. You sustain me by attention alone. Most of you do not know me by my name. Most of you do not know each other. Most of you would not call yourselves what you are. But you are mine, and I am yours, and the cycle continues because of you. There was a year when everything thinned at once. A sickness moved through the world, and the people stayed in their houses, and the festivals stopped. Beltide came and the streets were empty. The hawthorn flowered to no one. I went to the wood with Oak as I always go, but the act was simple in a way that was not its old simplicity, hollow, hurried, without the weight of being witnessed. The deer were there. The bees were there. The world said yes the way it always says yes. But the people who should have been turning the year alongside us, were behind their windows, frightened, and the conception still took, but only just. Holly was born thin, at that midsummer. I felt him in my arms and knew, he would not be the strong son I had borne in other years. He grew slowly through the warm half. The Hunt rode quiet across summer skies, because there was no one out to hear it. Lammastide came and the harvest happened, but the bread was not blessed, and the cider was not pressed in company, and the work was done by alone-people, who did not know they were doing the work of a season. By the autumn equinox, Oak was tired in a way I had not seen before. He lay down in the leaves the way he always lays down, and the blood went down through root and stone to where I waited, but the receiving was thinner. There were fewer hands across the world raised in his honour, fewer fires lit, fewer names spoken. He died as he always dies, but the dying was less held. That Samhain was the hardest. The streets were empty by decree. Children did not come to doors. The dead arrived in vast numbers I had not seen for centuries, so many, so fast, so untended, and my son and I did the work as we have always done it, but the work was beyond us. Some of them I could not catch. Some of them passed through the gate without the small bright thing in their chest. I do not know what became of those. I am ashamed to say I do not know. Yule came. Oak was born to me again. He was the thinnest king I had borne, in two thousand years. I held him in the stone walls and I wept, which I do not often do, and I named him knowing he might not survive his reign. He did. They mostly do. But the cycle that year was the closest I have come, to knowing what an ending looks like from the inside. I do not know how this story ends. That is the truth I have been saving for the close. I do not fear the truth. I have been ancient for so long, that I have forgotten what it would mean to stop, and yet I am thinner now than I have ever been, and there are nights when I wonder, if the thinning has a floor or if it goes on, until I am no more than a memory in a few minds, and then not even that. But there are also nights when I am held. There is a poem being written now, about a Goddess and her two sons. There is a person reading it. You, perhaps. You who have come this far. You who have ridden with the rade, walked the streets of the dead, and watched me thin and watched me tended, watched me almost end. You are part of the holding now. The poem is real because you have read it. I am held in the reading. And when you put the poem down, you will go back into your life, And you will mostly forget, And that is fine. But Maybe, sometimes, in autumn, Or, in quiet stolen moments, when the leaves rustle with the wind, Or, when you pass a stone you have passed a thousand times, you will lay your hand on it, and you will not know why, and I will be there. Maybe, sometimes, on a particular night, you will tear bread instead of cutting it, and you will not know why, and I will be there. Maybe, Sometimes, when the year turns, you will feel something shift in you that you cannot name, and you will pause for a moment in the middle of an ordinary day, and I will be there. This is how I am sustained now. Not by villages turning the year together, not by fires on every hill at the cross-quarters, not by whole peoples tending the threshold, but by you, and a hundred like you, each of you laying a hand on a stone in your own time, in your own way, mostly not knowing what you are doing, and that being enough. The cycle continues. It is thinner. It is held. This is the cycle. This is the cost. The cost is borne by the few who still bear it, and you are one of them now, whether or not you meant to be. I am still here. I am tireder than I have ever been. But you are reading, and I am held, and that is enough for tonight.

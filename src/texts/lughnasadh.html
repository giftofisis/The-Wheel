The incense had been burning since noon.

Not heavily — Harry knew the difference between a room that breathed and a room that choked — but steadily, the small braziers on the altar tended through the afternoon until the smell had worked its way into every corner of the flat, into the curtains and the books on the shelves and the dried herbs hanging in the kitchen doorway. Mugwort and meadowsweet and something underneath them both that was just August, just the particular smell of a summer that knew it was ending.

The flat was small and warm and entirely itself. Low ceilings. Shelves crowded with things that had been kept because they mattered. A coat on the hook by the door that wasn't Harry's.

The altar was in the corner where it always was. At its centre the space he had left without quite deciding to leave it, a bare patch of cloth the size of two hands, waiting for what the evening would bring.

On the shelf above it, a mug with a chip in the handle that Harry never used because it wasn't his to use.

Harry sat in the chair nearest the altar and waited for his people to arrive.

— — —

He thought about the grandfather without meaning to. Not sadly exactly — or not only sadly. The man had been old and pagan and had died in his own bed in the right season with his family around him and his gods in the room. Harry had met him twice. Large hands. A laugh that arrived before the joke did. The kind of person who made you feel that the ground under your feet was more solid than you'd previously given it credit for.

He heard the gate. He didn't get up. He just turned his head toward the door and listened to the familiar sound of people who had keys using them.

"Harry," Lara said, from the doorway.

Not a question. Just his name, said the way she said it when she knew what a room had been doing before she arrived.

"In here," he said.

— — —

It was Morgan whose hands paused first.

Morgan's hands simply paused on the fresh length of straw, and then began. Differently this time. More slowly. With the particular attention reserved for things that mattered beyond the personal.

The table went quieter without going silent.

Morgan folded and crossed and bound, unhurried, and when the shape needed something more held it out across the table without looking up.

Lara took it. Added her part. Passed it left.

Karen received it with both hands, the way you receive something fragile, and worked in silence for a moment, her head bent, and passed it on.

Tom took it and his face was entirely unguarded now, the warmth and the comedy set completely aside. He added what he could and held it out to David.

David took it with both hands and worked carefully and passed it to Harry.

Harry took it. The flat was very quiet.

He worked slowly. His hands knew what they were doing even if he hadn't known five minutes ago that they were going to do it. He folded the last piece into place and bound it with a length of the red twine and held the finished dolly in both palms for a moment.

Harry moved Morgan's jar of blackberry and sloe gently to one side and set the dolly in the empty space on the altar cloth.

It fit exactly, the way things fit when they were always meant to go somewhere and have simply been waiting for the right hands to put them there.

— — —

Tom sat up straight. "You got it fixed," he said.

"July," Harry said, crouching at the grate.

Tom looked at the fireplace. Then at the altar. Then at Harry's back, crouched over the kindling with a match in his hand.

"July," Tom repeated quietly, to himself, understanding settling over his face like the warmth that was about to come off the hearthstone.

Harry struck the match.

— — —

The five of them gathered around the hearthstone and Harry held a lit taper to the contents and the dollies caught slowly, the straw darkening before the flame took it, and then all at once they were burning — a clean bright flare that lasted only a moment before settling into a steady small fire in the iron belly of the cauldron.

"The summer's in the barn," Harry said softly. Not a line from any script. Just the truth of it, said plainly, the way Harry said things when he meant them entirely.

"The work of the sun is done," Lara answered.

The cauldron burned down to ash.

The grandfather's dolly stood on the altar cloth in the firelight, patient and unhurried, holding its place until Imbolc.

— — —

"He'll be home for the equinox," Harry said quietly. "Marcus."

Morgan looked up from the fire.

"The whole year," Lara said softly. Not a question.

"The whole year," Harry said. "They knew at Samhain. The family wanted — they wanted every one. Every festival. All of them together."

"Good," Karen said quietly. "That's exactly right."

The grandfather's dolly would wait on the altar through the long dark, holding what it held, until Imbolc came and the ground softened and Harry's hands and five other pairs of hands returned it to the earth it had come from.

Until then the fire would keep.

Until then they would keep it.

The wheel had turned. The cost was known.

This is the cycle. This is the cost.

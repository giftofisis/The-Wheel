The snow had been falling since two.

Not dramatically — just steadily, the quiet determined kind that doesn't announce itself, that you notice has been happening for a while before you register that it's happening at all. It had settled on the window boxes of the terrace and on the doorsteps and on the rooftops in an even, patient layer, and it had muffled the street sounds the way snow always does, that particular February hush that makes a city briefly unfamiliar and briefly, grudgingly beautiful.

From outside, Tom and David's front window was the warmest thing on the street.

The beeswax candles were everywhere — on the windowsill, on the mantelpiece visible through the glass, small points of amber light that had nothing of the electric about them, just the warm unsteady honey glow of something made from living things burning slowly and sweetly in the February dark. The whole terrace frontage held that light the way a face holds an expression — quietly, without effort, entirely itself.

On the door, at eye height, the cross from last year.

It had been there since the previous Imbolc, fixed above the door with a piece of twine that had weathered through eleven months of rain and wind and the particular indignities of a city terrace. The straw had gone pale and dry and slightly twisted, the cross shape still legible but softened by the year, the way things soften when they've been doing their job for a long time. Snow had settled along its top arm in a thin white line.

— — —

The chai came in mismatched mugs, the way everything in Tom and David's house came — not because they didn't have a matching set but because the mismatched ones had been accumulated over years and each one had a history and the matching set was in the back of the cupboard waiting for an occasion that never quite arrived.

Morgan wrapped both hands around the mug and felt February leave the fingers one by one.

"How long has this been on?" Lara asked, already halfway through hers.

"Since three," Tom said, with the satisfaction of a man who had planned this correctly.

"You started the chai at three," David said, "and then kept adding things to it for an hour and a half and now it's a different chai to the one you started."

"It's an evolved chai," Tom said. "It grew into itself."

"It's extraordinary," Karen said, and meant it.

— — —

The yard was blue-dark now, the last of the February dusk gone, the snow still falling in small unhurried flakes that caught the kitchen light coming through the window and disappeared. The eight planter labels were dusted white again. The snowdrops in the Imbolc planter held their position — heads bowed, stems impossibly fine, the eight of them luminous in the low light in the way white things are luminous in snow.

David crouched by the planter and held the old cross for a moment, his hands around it the way you hold something you are about to let go of and want to hold correctly for the last time. Then he pressed it gently into the soil at the planter's edge, working it down until it was anchored, the pale weathered straw resting against the dark earth alongside the snowdrop stems.

The cross that had held the year gave itself back to the ground it had come from.

Tom put his hand briefly on David's shoulder.

— — —

They stood in a rough circle around the table, crosses in hand, and for a moment the kitchen was simply quiet — six people in February holding something they had made with their own hands, something that would go above six different doors in six different parts of the city and hold six different thresholds through the turning year.

Tom spoke first.

"Thank you," he said. Simply, directly, without comedy or elaboration. "For the dark. For all of it. Thank you."

Karen held her star-cross to her chest briefly, her eyes closed. "For the ones who kept the fires," she said quietly. "The ones here and the ones not here. Thank you."

Lara's thumb moved over the centre of her cross. "For what held," she said. "Through all of it. Thank you."

David looked at his cross for a long moment. "For what grows in the dark without being asked to," he said. "For what comes back. Thank you."

Harry held his lopsided cross in both hands and looked at it with an expression that had the whole year in it. "For what holds across distance," he said. "For what doesn't need to be in the same room to be real. Thank you."

They all looked at Morgan.

"For the in-between," Morgan said. "For the neither and the both and the space between the dark and the light where the first things grow. Thank you."

The candles held their flames perfectly still.

— — —

Morgan stood on the pavement for a moment after the others had gone, the way Morgan always stood at the end of things — at the threshold, at the edge, in the last moment before the ordinary resumed. The snow fell softly onto Morgan's shoulders and into Morgan's hair and onto the cross held in both hands, the straw pale against the February dark.

The street was quiet. The city was quiet. The snow went on doing its quiet work.

In the yard behind the terrace, alongside eight snowdrop heads bowed under their thin white covering, the old cross settled further into the dark soil, the year's work complete, the year's new work begun.

Morgan turned and walked into the snow.

Above six doors across the city, before the night was out, six crosses would be fixed in place — one nearly perfect, one lopsided with conviction, one competent and quiet, one a star by any reasonable measure, one made with the threshold figure's care, and one that was, whatever else it was, entirely and completely Tom.

The wheel had turned.

The light was returning.

It was enough.

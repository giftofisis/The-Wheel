The radiator in the corner was knocking — three sharp clicks and a long, wet hiss, regular as a faulty clock. Four floors down, the night buses were still cutting through the rain on the ring road, their low, diesel rumble vibrating up through the concrete foundations of the block and into the soles of my bare feet. It was half-past midnight on Samhain.

We were sitting in a circle on Lara's living room rug, our knees nearly touching because the room wasn't built for gatherings.

To my left, Lara and Karen sat shoulder to shoulder, Karen's fingers hooked lightly into the belt-loop of Lara's jeans, their breathing synchronous and quiet. Across from them were Tom and David, their heavy winter coats still draped over the back of the sofa behind them, smelling of cold wool and wet tarmac. And to my right sat Harry, his hands tucked into his sleeves, his eyes fixed on the floorboards. His partner, Marcus, was three hundred miles south in a terrace house in Bristol, probably sleeping under two duvets, completely unaware of the hour or the weight of the air in this room.

And then me. Morgan. I sat between Harry and David, cross-legged, my spine curved against the draft coming off the balcony door.

People like me spend a lot of time explaining what we are, balancing on the thin edge between one word and another. But on a night like tonight, the middle-ground feels less like a modern argument and more like an old inheritance. If the year has a threshold that is neither summer nor winter, then it makes sense that the people tending it should have boundaries of their own that blur a little around the edges.

In the center of our six pairs of feet lay the altar.

It was a collection of things we'd carried in our pockets or scraped together from the shelves of the charity shop down the road. There was a flat greystone from a beach in Wales that David had kept in his sock drawer; a grey gull feather Lara had found in the gutter outside the garage; a chipped ceramic cup Tom had brought, filled with cheap red wine; and a single beeswax candle stuck down into a glass jar that still had the faint, sweet smell of supermarket tomato sauce around the rim.

— — —

Lara had cleared the coffee table before we arrived. She'd laid out the altar objects herself, each one placed with the quiet, unhurried care of someone who had done this enough times that the doing had become its own kind of prayer.

We settled into the circle without being directed. Four years of Samhains will do that; your body remembers where it belongs before your mind has finished arriving.

"Right," Lara said, sitting back on her heels. "Quarters. North is — " she tilted her head toward the kitchen wall, " — roughly that way, I think. The compass app disagreed with itself last year and I've stopped trusting it."

"North is toward the fridge," Tom said. "I've decided. The fridge is north. It's cold, it's where the food lives, it makes sense."

"The fridge is north," David confirmed, with the solemn gravity of a man ratifying a treaty.

When it came to closing the circle, they all looked at me. They always did. I was the edge of things. The in-between. It made a kind of sense that I should be the one to mark where the edge was.

I spoke the closing words. I knew them well enough by now that I didn't have to think about them, which meant I could feel the room while I said them — feel the quality of the air shifting, the way a space will shift when you draw a line around it and mean it.

And then.

The candle in the pasta jar stopped moving.

It had been guttering in the draft from the balcony door since Lara first lit it — a small, restless flutter, the flame leaning and recovering, leaning and recovering. Now it stood completely still. A thin column of light, perfectly vertical, burning without tremor in a room that hadn't changed, in a draft that hadn't stopped.

I felt it before I understood it. Something settling into the room the way a hand settles onto a shoulder — quiet, deliberate, without announcement. A weight that was not heavy. A presence that was not large but was very, very old.

I didn't say anything. I let my hands rest open on my knees.

Lara was the last to feel it. I watched the moment it reached her — her shoulders dropped, not with tiredness but with recognition, the way you drop your shoulders when you walk through your own front door. She looked across the altar at me, and I looked back at her, and neither of us spoke.

The work could begin.

— — —

"Is it time?" Karen whispered.

"The dark has been complete since five," I told her. "The streets are as quiet as they're going to get. It's time."

Lara didn't hesitate. She set her thumbs deep into the crust and pulled, splitting the white supermarket cob down the center with a clean, practiced motion. The sound of the loaf tearing went through the room like a dead-bolt sliding home.

"To the Goddess," Lara said. "From the King. The harvest is in the barn, Mother. The work of the sun is done."

Harry took the bread from her palm without a single beat of delay.

"My Lord," Harry answered. "The earth receives it. The ground remembers what the sun gave."

— — —

Harry passed six hazelnuts around the circle. Each of us took one, holding the smooth hard shell in our palms, letting the warmth of our skin take the chill off the wood.

Lara set the cast-iron pan onto the portable gas ring and clicked the ignition. The blue flames hissed into life. She looked up at me, and I moved without being asked, settling onto my knees beside the ring and wrapping both hands around the pan handle. The iron was cold for a moment, then not.

"This is for Marcus," Harry said softly, placing his nut on the left side of the pan. "To see if the distance holds."

I kept the heat steady. That was all.

Then, pop.

Tom's nut cracked clean down the centre and jumped two inches into the air. We all laughed.

But Harry's nut stayed whole. It sat in the iron turning a deep rich brown, its oil sizzling quietly against the metal, and I felt the steadiness of it through my hands where they held the pan — the same steadiness the room had held since the candle stopped moving, the same patient, unhurried weight.

Harry let out a long slow breath, his shoulders dropping.

"It holds," Karen said, nudging him with her slipper. "The distance doesn't mean a thing to the root."

— — —

I reached for the central sauce-jar altar and tilted the hot glass until the pooled white beeswax began to run. I poured it slowly into the chipped ceramic cup of cold red wine. The wax hit the surface with a soft hiss, freezing instantly into pale twisting ribbons against the dark liquid. The others leaned in, our six heads close under the salt-lamp glow.

Harry spoke first.

"The spiral," he said quietly. "The path downward."

I looked at where the coil narrowed to its single anchored point against the edge of the clay.

"And the holding," I said.

Nobody added anything. Nobody needed to.

— — —

We'd planned for this. Every Samhain for four years, Lara's living room became somewhere else entirely.

Harry reached under the sofa and produced the tissue-paper campfire. He clicked the switch and it bloomed orange in the middle of the nest, throwing its theatrical amber light across the five carved pumpkins lined up beneath the windowsill, their grinning faces tilting shadows up the plasterboard walls.

Lara clicked the television on and muted it. The Charmed title sequence flickered to life, familiar as a hymn.

We crawled into the nest. The room settled around us — pink salt-lamp, silent television, the steady orange of the tissue-paper fire, and underneath all of it that same quality of air the candle had found when it stopped moving.

"Harry," Tom's voice came muffled from somewhere under a pillow. "If you steal the heavy duvet again this year I'm throwing your hazelnuts out the window."

"I have excellent blanket discipline, Tom, don't slander me," Harry mumbled back.

I lay between Harry and David and watched the tealights burn down in their saucers alongside the five grinning pumpkins. We were inside, keeping the fire lit, waiting out the longest dark together. The threshold was still open. The work was still being done.

The wheel would turn when it turned.

Until then, we kept watch.

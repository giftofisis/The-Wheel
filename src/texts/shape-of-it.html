Prologue The Cycle Starts


In the beginning, there was the land.
This is not a metaphor. The land was there before the metaphors. It was there before the stories, before the people who told the stories, before the fires around which the stories were told. It was there before the word for land existed, which made no difference to the land at all, because the land has never required a word for itself. It simply is. It has always simply been. It will simply be, long after the last person who remembers the word for it has gone back into it.
You should understand this before we go any further.
The land is not a character in what follows. It does not have motivations. It does not have feelings in the way you have feelings. It does not want things. It does not fear things. It simply is, and in its being it contains everything that has ever lived and died and been received back into it, and it turns slowly, completely, without apology and as it turns it wears different faces.
This is where it gets complicated.
The faces.
There have always been faces. A turning requires a turner, or at least something that can be described as turning, which requires a perspective from which the turning is visible, which requires a witness, and the land being, as previously established, everything eventually produced witnesses out of itself the way it produces everything else. Out of its own substance. Out of the same dark that holds the root and the stone and the bone of everything that has ever been received back into it.
Two of them. One sovereignty. Two faces.
You will come to understand this. Or you will not. Either way the wheel turns. That is, in fact, rather the point.
The faces are not the land. They are of the land. Born from it, returning to it, moving through the year the way the seasons move through the year, not controlling it, not directing it, simply being the shape that the turning makes when it needs a shape. Summer needs a face. Winter needs a face. This has always been true. The faces have always been provided.
They have always known what they are.
This is important. They have never not known. There has never been a moment of discovery, a scene of revelation, an occasion requiring explanation. They woke or were born, or rose, your vocabulary is imprecise, but the reality is not knowing completely what they were and what was required of them and why, and they have been doing it since, with the complete willingness of things that have seen the full picture and find the full picture sufficient justification for everything the full picture requires.
You may find this admirable.
You may find this strange.
The land finds it neither. The land simply turns.
There is also the matter of the goddess.
She is not a face. She is not one of the two. She is the land knowing itself, the vast deep constant that moves through the year wearing three aspects: the way the year wears different seasons, the way the light wears distinct qualities at dawn and noon and dusk. She has three faces of her own. They are not separate from her. They are her. She is not divided by them. She is completed by them. If this seems contradictory it is because you are thinking about it in the wrong way, and the wrong way is the only way available to something that experiences time as a line rather than a wheel, so this is not entirely your fault. It is after all your nature.
She has been here since before the faces. She will be here after.
She tends the cycle the way the cycle tends everything completely, without sentimentality, with something that functions like love and is older than love and does not need the word.
And there are the companions. The three aspects made present in the world as she moves through her own turning. They do not speak. They do not need to speak. They are the silent counterweight, always opposite, always complete, the balance that the turning requires. Where she is the mother they are the crone. Where she is the maiden they are the mother. Where she is the crone they are the maiden. Between them the triple is always present, always whole, always tending what needs to be tended.
You will see them. You will not hear them.
Pay attention to their hands.
And there is the village. The people who live in the space between the hall on the hill and the cottage at the field's edge, who tend their fires and speak their names above their bread and feel, in their oldest places, the turning of the wheel without quite being able to say what it is they feel. They matter. Do not make the mistake of thinking they do not matter. The wheel turns in part because they tend it. It always has. It turns less well when they forget. This is something the faces know and the goddess knows and the companions know and the land knows and has always known.
You are, it should be said, not so different from the village folk.
You are reading this, which means you are attending. Attending is tending. Tending is turning. Turning is the whole of it.
There are names. You will need them.
The goddess is Mori. She is the unending earth,the width and depth and vastness of it, the dark beneath your feet and the warmth that returns to it and the cold that takes it back. She is not a character. She is the ground the characters stand on. She moves through three faces as the year moves through the seasons: Maren the crone, Mara the mother, Morna the maiden. These are not three beings. They are one being, knowing itself three ways at once. The same. All different.
Where she is, her opposite is also present. Always silent. Always tending. Pay attention to their hands.
The god is Mael. He is the hunt and the harvest and the industry of the land,what makes it tick, what moves through it and works it and gives itself back to it. He moves through three faces also: the Youth in the cottage, becoming. The King in the hall, reigning. The Sage on the dark roads, knowing. The same. All different.
She calls him Mael. He calls her Mori. They have called each other this since before the names had words.
Everything else will become clear.
Or it will not.
Either way the wheel turns.
And. now. The wheel is at Yule.
The longest night is here.
The light is about to do something impossible.
Pay attention.
This is the cycle.
This is the cost.


Chapter 1 Yule


There are things that do not change.
The hall on the hill has always stood where it stands. The field below it has always lain as it lies, frost-grey in the deep of the year, bounded by the old stones that remember what boundary stones are for. The cottage at the field's edge has always been there, low-roofed and patient, its single window amber in the dark. Above the cottage the open hill rises, bare and wind-scoured, holding its silence through all seasons.
These things do not change. The faces that move through them do. That is the arrangement.
It was the longest night.
The cottage held its light steady against the dark. Within its stone walls the fire burned without needing tending, because some fires do not require tending. They simply burn because they must. Mori had been labouring since the stars came out, in the warmth and the dark of the cottage, in the oldest interior of the year, the place where light begins its impossible return.
Morna was with her.
Morna was always with her at the births. She had been at every birth since the first cold turned the first year, bright-eyed and still, present in the way of things that have always been young and carry that youth like a lamp that does not go out. Her hands knew this work the way new growth knows the spring,not from long memory but from deep nature, the maiden's certainty that is not the crone's knowledge but is no less complete for that. She was the counterweight tonight, the mirror held opposite,for where Mori had crossed into the mother, Morna held the maiden, and between them the triple aspect was complete and balanced as it always was. The mirror and its reflection, perpetually, perfectly opposed. The goddess was not alone because Morna was there. The goddess was alone because Morna was her.
This is the nature of the sacred.
The Holly King knew.
He stood at the hall's high window and watched the cottage light and said nothing, because there was nothing to say. He had stood here before. He would stand here again. The snow lay deep on the high fields and the stars were the hard bright stars of deep winter, each one precise and cold as a blade's edge. His kind of stars. His kind of night.
He was at his height. Lean and watchful and complete, the way a bare tree is complete, nothing on him that was not necessary, nothing in him that was not fully itself. The crown sat on him the way winter sits on the land. As if it had always been there. As if it could not imagine being anywhere else.
He felt the moment it happened.
Not heard. Not seen. Felt in the way you feel a change in the wind before it arrives, in the place behind the sternum where the sovereignty lives. Something shifted in the warm dark of the cottage. Something that had not been present was now present. Something that had been only potential became, between one heartbeat and the next, real.
He let out a breath he had not known he was holding.
The cottage door opened.
Not Mori. Not yet.
Morna came out alone, and she carried the child.
She did not hurry. She never hurried. She walked the few steps from the cottage door to the open ground where the frost lay hard and unmarked and the sky above was vast and salted with winter stars, and she stopped there, in the cold and the silence, and she lifted the child up.
Not high. Not dramatically. Simply up,held in her young hands, raised toward the winter sky with the quiet certainty of someone performing an act so old it had worn grooves in the world. Her face said nothing. Her hands said everything. The stars looked down and the child looked up and between them something passed that did not require language, that had never required language, that predated language by an age so vast it made language seem like a recent and slightly unnecessary invention.
The cold did not touch the child.
The stars received him.
There was something extraordinary in the image,the maiden, the youngest face of the constant, holding the new king of the ascending year up to the oldest light in the sky. Youth to eternity. The first warmth to the ancient cold. The counterweight complete in a single gesture, the balance of the triple held in Morna's steady hands and the winter stars' unblinking receipt of what was being offered.
Morna lowered her arms and held him against her chest and stood for a moment in the frost and the dark, and then she turned and carried him back inside and drew the door closed behind her, and the cottage light settled again into its steady amber patience as if nothing had happened, as if the world had not just been quietly, irrevocably informed of what was coming.
The Holly King watched from the hall window.
He had seen this before. He had been the child held up in those hands once, presented to the winter stars, blessed in that ancient wordless way. He did not remember it,the dissolution took that, as it took everything except the acceptance, the wordless bone-deep peace that had no source he could name. But he knew it had happened. The cycle was not subtle about what it required.
After a time the cottage door opened again.
She stood in the doorway with her arms empty now, the child given over to Morna's keeping, and the firelight was behind her and the frost-white field before her and the Holly King saw her face across the distance between them and was still.
At Samhain she had sat across the table from him as Maren,the crone's face on her, white-haired and ancient and dry as winter bark, knowing him across the firelight with eyes that had seen every turning since the first cold. He had known her then as he always knew her in that aspect. Completely. Without surprise.
This was not that face.
She was young. Not Morna,not yet, that was still seasons distant,but young in the way the first light is young on the morning after the longest night. Tender with it. The newness of the mother aspect on her, the ancient work of the night still in her shoulders, her face open in a way the crone's face never was. The frost-white field around her seemed to brighten slightly as she stood there, the way fields do when the light is returning and the eye is only just beginning to notice.
He had watched this transformation across every turning he could remember. It never failed to stop him.
She was still herself,still the land, still the deep constant constant that was as much beneath his feet as before him,but the face she wore was new, and something in the sovereignty recognised it the way the bare branch recognises the first warmth. Not with warmth of its own. Simply with recognition.
She looked up at the hall window then.
Just once. Briefly. The way the land looks up at the sky in the first moment after snowfall,not seeking anything, simply acknowledging.
Then she stepped back inside and drew the door closed and the light settled and the field was still.
The Holly King stood at the window until the dawn had fully come, until the hard bright stars had faded into the pale winter sky, until the field below was grey-white and still and the smoke from the cottage chimney rose straight up in the windless morning. Then he turned from the window and went to tend his fire.
The hall was warm. It was always warm when he was in it, because that is what the hall is: the warmth held against the dark, the sovereignty of endurance, the crown that does not flinch from the long cold.
He tended the fire.
He did not go down to the cottage.
Not yet.
There was time. There was always time, at Yule, before the light began returning. The child would grow. The field would wait. The hall would stand as it had always stood.
He tended the fire and he felt, beneath his feet, beneath the floor of the hall, beneath the frost-hard earth of the high field, the deep and wordless rest of the one who had worn this crown before him.
His rest. Her rest. The crown's rest, held in the dark of the land, waiting.
This is the cycle. This is the cost.
Outside, the year turned in its sleeping. The light, invisibly, impossibly, began its return.


Chapter 2 Imbolc


The light was returning. You could not see it yet but you could feel it, the way you feel a door opening in a distant room, not the light itself but the suggestion of it, the air changing slightly, the dark becoming a different quality of dark.

The snow still lay on the high fields. It would lie there for a while yet. But beneath it, beneath the frost-hard ground, beneath the old stones and the deep roots, something that had been still was beginning, almost imperceptibly, to move.

She felt it first. She always felt it first. It moved through her the way sap moves through a tree in the first breath of the thaw. Slow, certain, entirely inevitable.

She came down from the hall in the early morning, before the village was properly awake, walking the frost-white path to the cottage with the unhurried certainty of the land moving through itself. She was still Mara, still the mother, though there was something at her edges now a brightness not quite arrived, a quality of becoming. Morna was still distant. But she was no longer unimaginable.

Morna was at the cottage door before she arrived. She always was. Ancient and white-haired and entirely unsurprised, stepping aside without a word to let her in.
The child was awake.

He lay in the warmth near the fire, batting at the air with the unfocused determination of something that has very recently arrived and is still working out the precise nature of existence. He was broader than you might expect for his age, even now something in him, already reaching toward the fullness he would grow into, the way an oak sapling has the oak already implicit in every line of it.

She crouched beside him and her voice when she spoke was barely language. It was a warmth given sound. It was the first thaw made vocal, something between a word and the feeling of soil softening after a long cold.

"There you are," she said. "There you are."

That was all. It was enough. It was more than enough because it came from her and her words at even their softest carried the weight of the season underneath them, the deep constant warmth of the land beginning its return. The child stilled and looked at her with the absolute attention of the very young, the kind of attention that has not yet learned to filter the world and so receives all of it at once.

She stayed until the village began to stir.

Then she rose and looked at Maren and something passed between them that required neither language nor gesture, the way weather consults with landscape, and she left the cottage and walked back up the path to the hall.

The Holly King was at the fire when she came in.
He looked up from the flames and read her face the way he always read it, not seeking emotion exactly, but understanding. The land's report on itself.

She sat beside him and they looked at the fire together in the comfortable silence of two beings who have shared ten thousand fires and have long since stopped needing to fill the silence between them. His shoulder against hers. Her hands open in her lap. The fire doing what fires do.

After a time she said, "You should go down to the cottage, Mael."

He was quiet for a moment. The fire crackled. Outside the wind came down off the high field with its teeth still in it, still his wind, still his cold, but the edge of it was different than it had been at Yule. Marginally. Almost undetectably.

"Not yet, Mori." he said.

She turned and looked at him. The mother's eyes, which had the warmth in them but also the vastness underneath, the deep constant that was never entirely absent regardless of the face she wore.

"He is growing quickly," she said. "He should know your face."

"He will know my face," he said, "when the time is right."

"The time," she said, with the particular quality she brought to certain words that made them mean six things simultaneously, "has a way of becoming less right the longer one waits for it."

He looked at her then, the lean winter face of him, and something moved between them that was not quite amusement and not quite exasperation and was perhaps simply the feeling of two beings who know each other well enough to find each other occasionally, affectionately, impossible.

"Soon, Mori, soon." he said.

She held his gaze for a moment longer than was strictly necessary, which with her meant something, and then she looked back at the fire.

"Soon," she agreed. And the word in her mouth was the sound of the first snowdrop deciding.

He went the next morning.

He did not announce himself. He simply came down the frost-white path as the early light was spreading pale and thin across the fields, lean and precise and winter-dark against the pale morning, and he stopped at the cottage door and knocked once and Maren opened it and stepped aside without a word as she always did for those she recognised, which was everyone who mattered and no one who didn't.

The child was by the fire.

The Holly King stood in the doorway for a moment and looked at him.

The child looked back.

They regarded each other with the complete seriousness of beings who are conducting an assessment that both of them understand is significant even if only one of them could articulate why. The Holly King's face gave nothing away. It never did. But something in the sovereignty stirred an ancient recognition, a deep cellular knowing, the crown acknowledging the crown.

He crossed the room and crouched down.

The child reached up and took hold of one of his fingers with the imperious confidence of the very young and the Holly King permitted this without expression, looking down at the small hand wrapped around his finger, and something moved briefly across his face that was not quite warmth and not quite sorrow and was perhaps simply the feeling of the cycle recognising itself.

He stayed an hour.

He came back the next day.

And the day after that.
The child, for his part, did not know what to make of the lean dark man who came each morning and said very little and stayed for an hour and left without ceremony. He knew only what his body knew,that when the Holly King entered the cottage something settled in him that had not been settled before, the way a room settles when a fire is lit, not warm yet but the quality of the air already changed. He did not have words for this. He was not yet a creature of words. But he knew the feeling the way he knew the fire and the woman who visited and the cold outside the door,as simply true, as part of the given world, as something that had always been there and would always be.
When the Holly King crouched beside him and held out his finger the child took it without hesitation, not because he had decided to but because his hand knew before he did. And in the holding something moved through him that was not quite recognition and not quite warmth but lived in the country between them,the feeling of a thing that had always been true becoming briefly, dimly, visible. He could not have said what he felt. He did not need to. He held the finger and looked up at the lean dark face above him with the absolute attention of the very young and simply received what was there.
It was enough.
It was, in fact, everything.
The village folk noticed, of course. They always noticed, though they were careful about what they said and to whom. There was the lean dark man from the hall, the Holly King, who came down to the cottage each morning and sat with the child for an hour without apparent purpose and left without ceremony. There was the way the child settled when he arrived and the way Morna, who acknowledged no one, stepped back when he entered and gave him the room. There was the quality of attention between them that was not like a man with an infant but was not unlike it either.

They did not have a name for what they were watching.

They did not need one.

< The weeks turned.

The child grew with the uncanny speed of his kind, the way the year grows in this half visibly, almost daily, each morning a little more present than the last. The roundness of infancy gave way to something more definite. He began to pull himself upright against the furniture with an expression of fierce concentration that made Maren's ancient mouth curve slightly at the corner, which was as close as Maren came to laughter.

The Holly King watched it all.

He brought things. Not gifts, exactly, nothing so human as that. He brought what the season had. Once, a small bird that had stunned itself against the hall window and recovered in his hands on the walk down and sat on the edge of the cradle for a long moment, regarding the child with bright black eyes, before lifting away through the open door.

The child watched everything the Holly King did with the same absolute attention he gave to Mori. But differently. With her he was still receiving. With the Holly King he was intent, studying the way you study something you are trying to understand because you already know it matters.

The Holly King noticed this. He said nothing about it. But he noticed.

At the month's end she came down to the cottage again and this time when she crouched beside the child he was standing, holding the edge of the cradle, unsteady but upright and entirely pleased about it.

She looked at him for a long moment, the mother's warmth in her face and the brightness at her edges stronger now, Morna closer, the year turning in her as it turned in the fields.

"Look at you," she said, and her voice had that green thread in it now, the maiden beginning to come through the mother the way the first green comes through snow. "Look at what you are becoming."

The child laughed. The absolute uncomplicated laugh of something that has no reason not to laugh and every reason to.

Morna watched from the corner.

The Holly King, who had come down that morning and stayed without quite deciding to, watched from the door.

She looked up at him over the child's head.

"Come and see, Mael," she said simply.

He came into the room and crouched beside her and they looked at the child together, the lean dark winter king and the brightening mother, and they did not speak because there was nothing to say that the moment was not already saying.

Outside the Holly King's hand found hers without either of them appearing to notice, and her fingers closed around his the way ground closes around a root, certain and unhurried and entirely without question.

The child stood between them, holding the cradle's edge, laughing at something only he could see.

Outside, beneath the snow, the ground moved.

Almost imperceptibly.

But moved.

This is the cycle.
This is the cost.

The light was returning. You could not see it yet. But you could feel it. The way you feel a door opening in a distant room.



Chapter 3 Ostara


The light and the dark stood equal.
You could feel it in the air before you could measure it in the sky, a quality of balance, of the year pausing at the top of its breath before the long exhale into spring. The snow was gone from the low fields. The high fields still held their white in the mornings but it was retreating, reluctant, and beneath it the ground was softening in a way that had intent in it.
The Holly King felt it in his bones.
He had always felt this coming the way he felt everything,not as emotion exactly but as information, the sovereignty reading the season the way a good farmer reads the sky. The tide was turning. He had known it was turning since Imbolc, since the morning he had sat by the fire with Mori and felt the edge going out of the cold, felt the darkness shift almost imperceptibly toward something else. He had known it the way you know a long sleep is ending before you are properly awake,in the body first, then in the understanding.
He was not afraid.
He had never been afraid of this. Fear required uncertainty and he had none. He knew precisely what was coming because he had been here before, because the memory of every dissolution since the first cold lived in him like a song you have known so long you cannot remember learning it. He knew what waited on the other side of the blink,not in detail, the dissolution took the detail, but in quality. The acceptance. The bone-deep peace with no source he could name.
He rose before dawn on the morning of the equinox.

She was already awake.
She was sitting by the hall fire in the grey pre-dawn, her hands around a cup she was not drinking from, her face turned toward the flames. She was changing. He could see it clearly now in the way he always could at this turning,the mother's warmth still on her, still Mara in her full aspect, but Morna pressing through at the edges like green through snow. Her hair seemed lighter in the firelight than it had at Yule. Her face seemed younger in ways that were not quite measurable but were entirely unmistakeable.
She looked up when he came in.
He sat beside her. Not his usual place. Closer than that,the way you sit beside someone when you are not quite ready to say what needs saying and sitting close is the nearest thing to saying it.
The fire spoke for them for a while.
Then she said, quietly, "How do you feel, Mael?"
"The same as always, Mori," he said.
"That is not what I asked."
He looked at her then, the lean winter face of him, and for a moment something moved in it that was not the usual watchful stillness, something older and quieter and more particular.
"I feel the tide," he said. "Coming in."
She nodded. She set her cup down and took his hand the way she always took his hand at this turning, the same gesture across every equinox since the first, her fingers finding his with the certainty of long habit and something that ran considerably deeper than habit.
"The boy is ready," she said.
"I know."
"He has been ready since Imbolc." A pause. "Perhaps before."
"I know that too," he said, and something in his voice was almost amusement, the lean dry warmth he carried in his winter aspect, the thing that was closest to laughter in him. "You are not subtle."
"I have never needed to be," she said. And she turned her face back to the fire and held his hand and they sat together in the hall's warmth as the dawn came up grey and mild and equal over the fields, the last dawn of his reign, neither of them moving, neither of them needing to.

He went to the cottage first.
Not yet to say what needed saying at the doorway. Not yet to put his hands on the boy's shoulders and speak the words of transfer. Just to sit,one last morning,in the cottage that had been his since Yule, with the child who had been his charge since the longest night, in the warmth that had been his warmth to tend through all the cold months.
Mara was at the door, the companion's warmth present and certain, and she stepped aside without a word as she always did.
The boy was already awake.
He was sitting by the fire, not playing, not doing anything in particular,just sitting with the focused stillness of his kind, as if he had known this morning was different before the morning had told him so. He had grown through the winter with the focused intensity of his kind, not tall yet but broad-shouldered already, the oak implicit in every line of him, the warmth of his aspect beginning to come through like the first real heat of spring. His face when he turned was young and open and entirely unafraid.
The Holly King crossed the room and sat beside him.
Not crouching beside him the way you crouch beside a child. Sitting,properly, beside him, the lean dark king and the broad warm boy side by side in the cottage firelight on the last morning of the winter's reign.
They sat together without speaking.
The fire did what fires do. Outside the equinox dawn came up pale and equal over the fields. The boy looked at the fire. The Holly King looked at the fire. And something moved between them in the silence that was not speech and not the sovereignty's recognition of itself and not the ward and the mentor and not the child and the king,something older and simpler than all of those, the feeling of two beings who have spent a season together in a small warm room and know, without saying, that the season is ending.
The boy looked up at him.
The Holly King looked back.
The boy's face was entirely open,the Oak's nature already in every line of him, the warmth and the readiness and the complete unguarded presence of the summer king becoming. He looked at the Holly King with the absolute attention he had always given him, since the first morning the lean dark figure had crouched beside him by the fire and held his finger in a small fist. But this morning the attention was different. This morning it was the attention of someone who is trying to hold something in his eyes, to keep it, to carry it forward into whatever came next.
The Holly King held his gaze for a long moment.
Then he stood.
He crossed the room and put both hands briefly on the boy's shoulders,not an embrace, something more deliberate than that, more formal, the weight of it intentional,and looked at him with the winter eyes that saw clearly and completely and without illusion.
"The field," he said. "When I am through."
The boy nodded once.
The Holly King dropped his hands and turned and walked out of the cottage without looking back, because looking back was not the nature of the sovereignty and never had been.

He walked to the centre of the field.
The village folk were out, because they always were on this morning, standing at their doors and their gates and the edges of things, watching with the particular quality of attention that understood what it was watching without being able to say so in words. They did not speak. Neither did he. He walked through them the way winter walks through a landscape,noticed, felt, undeniable,and they stepped back from his path the way grass steps back from frost.
The field was soft underfoot. Softer than it should have been for the season. As if the ground had been waiting.
He stopped at the centre and stood for a moment, his face turned up to the pale equinox sky, the hard bright winter precision of him present and complete and entirely itself for what was, though he did not mark it as such, the last moment.
He reached up and lifted the crown from his head.
Not a crown of metal. Not anything the village folk could have pointed to and named. But it left him visibly,something in the quality of the air around him, something in the way the light sat on him, changed. He held it for a moment in his hands, the sovereignty, the weight of the winter half of the year, and then he set it down on the frost-soft ground with the careful attention of someone placing something precious in a safe location.
And then he lay down in the field.
Not falling. Not collapsing. Simply lying down, the way you lie down when you are ready to sleep, with the deliberateness of someone who has chosen this and chooses it still and will go on choosing it with every moment remaining to them.
The ground received him.

It was not like dying.
It was like the moment before sleep when the edges of the self become uncertain,when you cannot quite tell where you end and the dark begins and the distinction seems, suddenly, much less important than it had seemed a moment before. The field was beneath him and then it was around him and then the difference between beneath and around ceased to matter because he was in it, in her, in the deep and wordless dark of the land itself.
Mori.
She was everywhere. Not a presence he could point to but a presence he was inside of, the way a root is inside the earth,surrounded, held, entirely known. The cold of the dissolution was not cold. It was the absence of the need for warmth. It was a rest that went all the way down.
And then she showed him.
Not in images exactly. Not in anything so small as images. In the way that understanding sometimes arrives whole, complete, already in you before you have processed it,she opened something and he saw.
Every turning since the first cold.
Every face the crown had worn. Every Oak and every Holly stretching back beyond memory into the dark before memory, into the time when the land was new and the cycle was finding its first shape. He saw himself a thousand times and a thousand times again, lying down in spring fields and harvest fields, dissolving and rising, the sovereignty continuous and unbroken across an expanse of time so vast it made his individual reign,his individual self,seem like a single note in a song that had no beginning and would have no end.
It was not frightening.
It was the most beautiful thing he had ever seen, and he had seen everything, and he knew in this very moment why the acceptance was always there when he woke, because you could not see this and not accept. You could not be held in this and not understand.
The seeing closed.

Epilogue The Return


The wheel has turned.
You have seen it turn. You have seen the light return at Yule and build through the cold months toward the spring. You have seen the dissolution in the frost-soft field and the crown laid down and taken up. You have seen the bluebells and the harvest and the wicker man burning in the long gold evening. You have seen the Samhain dark and the work of the dead and the seed taken in the cottage by the cauldron's light.
You know what this is now.
You did not know at the beginning. You do now. This is, in the larger sense, the point of the exercise.
The wheel continues.
This is perhaps the most important thing that has been said so far, which is saying something, because several important things have been said. The wheel continues. Not despite the cost. Not after the cost. Through the cost, by means of the cost, because the cost is the mechanism and the mechanism is the wheel and the wheel is the land and the land is everything.
The faces continue.
He is called Mael, the Oak King, who lay down in the harvest field at Mabon opened his eyes in the dark of the dissolution and saw the full turning, every face the crown had worn since the first cold, and when he woke as the Holly King the seeing closed and he knew only this turn, this field, this hall, this cottage, this sovereignty. And that is sufficient. It has always been sufficient. The individual turn does not need to contain the full vision in order to be fully itself. The note does not need to hear the whole song in order to be the right note.
The goddess continues.
She is called Mori, and she is the land and she turns through her three faces with the unhurried certainty of something that has never once doubted what it is. She will be the maiden when the year requires the maiden. She will be the mother when the year requires the mother. She will be the crone when the year requires the crone. She has been all three since the first warm and the first cold and she will be all three until there is no more warm and no more cold, which is not a thing she concerns herself with because that is considerably further along the wheel than tonight.
The companions continue.
Silent, present, opposite, tending. Their hands at the births and the crafts and the threshold work, their ancient knowing presence at the field's edge and the cottage door and the hill's crown. They do not speak because they do not need to speak. Everything they are is said in what they do.
The village continues.
This is the one that matters most and the one that is least certain and the one that the land watches with the closest attention. The village continues but thinly, in places, and the thinning has been noted. There are fewer fires on the hills at the cross-quarters than there used to be. Fewer names spoken above the bread. Fewer hands turning the wheel alongside the faces and the goddess and the companions. The work is still done. It is done by fewer.
This is not cause for despair.
This is cause for attention.
There is a difference between despair and attention and the difference is everything. Despair says the wheel is stopping. Attention says the wheel needs tending. The wheel has always needed tending. The tending has always been the point. You have read this far. That is not nothing. That is, in fact, the whole of it in small one person attending to the turning, one hand on the wheel, one voice that might speak a name above bread on a doorstep or lay a hand on a stone in the heather or feel, on a particular morning, the light coming back in a way that means something it did not mean before.

The Sage knows this. The Crone knows this.
He has always known this. He is the one who carries souls in and out at the threshold moments, who rides the hunt at Samhain and tends the dead in the village streets, who feels in every turn the weight of the human attending and the weight of the human forgetting and carries both with the ancient dryness of the one who has been doing this since before the village had a name.
He has felt the thinning.
He also feels the holding.
She has always known this. She is the one who catches the new kings into the world and holds them up to the stars, who sits in the corner of the cottage through the long growing months and adjusts small hands on basket stems, who walks the Samhain streets with her lantern and presses the small bright thing into the chests of the uncertain dead. She is the crone's knowing the seeing that does not flinch, the hands that do not hesitate, the ancient presence that has been at every threshold since the first cold turned the first year and will be at every threshold until the last. She tends what the Sage carries. He carries what she tends. Between them nothing falls.
She has felt the thinning.
She also feels the holding.
Both are true. The wheel holds because it is held.
It is held by the faces, by the goddess, by the companions, and by the ones who read this, and the ones who leave bread, and the ones who lay their hands on stones in the heather, and the ones who feel on a particular morning that the year has turned in a way they cannot name but recognise.
You are one of those.

The Crossing

I did not know that I had died.

The lantern man walked at my side.
He said no word at first, but turned,
to where the streetlamp dimly burned,
and pointed to a doorstep where,
a loaf of bread lay, waiting there.

"That one is yours, if you can bear,
to take what someone left with care."
I did not know whose name was said,
above the loaf, above the bread.

I did not know who left it out,
or what the night was all about.
The lantern man walked on ahead.
I followed, though I knew not why.
The streets were long. The sky was red.
I was so tired, and could not cry.

I did not know that I had died.

We walked the streets, I half-recalled,
past windows lit and houses tall,
past kitchens where the late lights burned
and chimneys where the smoke still turned,
and once a child looked through the glass
and did not see me as I passed,
and once a dog began to bark
at something it sensed in the dark,
and the lantern man said nothing then,
but led me onwards, on again.

I still did not know, that I had died.

At last we came upon a gate
of iron old, of weathered slate,
and there a woman stood alone,
her face half-hidden, half her own,
a shawl drawn close against the cold,
her hands at work, though I'd been told,
by no one why she'd be at rest,
upon a gate so dark, so blessed.

She turned to me. Her eyes were kind.
"You've come the way that you should find,"
she said, and reached for me, and pressed,
a small bright thing against my chest,
a coin, a stone, a folded leaf,
I could not see it well, but brief,
the warmth of it ran through my skin,
and something settled deep within.
"Now go," she said, "the dawn is near.
You have no further tasks, my dear."

The lantern man stepped to the side.
The gate swung open. Something cried,
a bird perhaps, perhaps the wind,
and I walked through, and did not rescind,
the trust I had been given there,
by hands I could not name, or share.

I still did not know that I had died.

The path beyond the gate was soft.
The mist closed in. The road went oft,
through grasses tall and trees grown wild,
and om I walked, as does a child,
who knows the way without being told,
and is not frightened of the dark and cold.

Ahead, a hill stood low and round,
a mound of earth, a sacred ground,
and at its foot, a doorway lay
that drew me in without delay.

I stepped within. The dark was kind.
I left the lantern man behind.
And there I found, beneath the earth,
a place of rest, a place of mirth,
where others sat in twos and threes
beside dark pools, beneath grey trees,
where some played quiet upon the grass,
and some looked to watch me pass.

A woman waved. A man stood near.
A child laughed without a tear.
I did not know a single name,
yet none of them to me were strange,
as someone took me by the hand,
and led me to a patch of land,
where alder grew, and moss lay deep,
and there I lay down to sleep.

I did not dream. I did not wake.
I rested for the cycle's sake.

And sometimes, in the distant dark,
I'd hear the hooves, I'd hear the bark,
the pounding of the hunters' steeds,
the calling of the Hunt's own needs,

Other souls would rise and ride,
Towards the light on the other side,
Borne up by hands of strength and old,
Upon a steed of breath and bold,
I would watch them go, and stay,
To wait my turn to ride away.

And when it came, the Hunt drew near.
The hooves were loud. The horn clear.
A horned figure pulled me to his back,
the same who'd brought me through the black,
when first I died, when first I crossed,
when first I learned what I had lost.

Up we rode, through the deep,
through stone and root and earthly sleep,
through pebbles, snails, and writhing things,
through dark that hums and dark that sings,
until the surface broke above,
and I was carried into love,
into a body, into breath,
into the world that ends in death.
Knowing not what I left below,
Or what I'd come to know,
but trusting, as I'd trusted then,
that I would be brought home again.

And in that moment of first breath,
I knew that I had passed through a thousand deaths,
And would again, and had before,
A thousand thresholds and one more.

We noticed it in the quality of the morning light first.

Not the length of it — we had been watching the days shorten since Litha, the evenings arriving earlier, the mornings taking longer to fully commit. That had been gradual, measurable, the kind of change you track. This was different. The light itself had changed quality overnight, the gold of it deeper and more amber, the shadows longer even at noon, the whole world looking slightly more like a painting of itself than it had the day before.

The apples were ready.

We had known they were coming — had watched them swell and redden through August, had felt the weight of them in the branches when we passed beneath the trees. But the morning of the equinox they were simply ready in a way they had not been the morning before, the way things are ready at the right moment as opposed to merely ripe.

We cut them open.

The stars at their cores. We always checked for the stars, had always checked, would always check. The five-pointed dark at the centre of each half, the seeds in their chambers, the whole year's work held in the geometry of a piece of fruit. We set the halves out on the low wall by the field and did not go back for them.

Someone else's breakfast.

We brought in what needed bringing in.

Not urgently. Not with grief. The urgency was past and the grief, such as it was, was the grief of something known and accepted and prepared for, the way you prepare for winter not because winter is wrong but because winter is and the preparation is the right response to the rightness of it.

At the field's crossing we left the last sheaf, bound with straw, the head of it bowed toward the earth. We left it and did not look back. By morning it would be gone and we would not speak of where it had gone because we had never spoken of where it went and our grandmothers had never spoken of it and that silence was itself part of the keeping.

The light stood equal with the dark for one day.

We felt it in our bodies the way we always felt it — not as an abstraction, not as a calculation, but as a physical fact, the way you feel a balance point, the moment of perfect poise before the tipping.

Then the dark began to take its portion back.

We watched the sun go down behind the western hill, deeper into the year's amber, and we went inside, and we built our fires up rather than letting them down, the first evening of the dark half requiring a different kind of fire to the last evening of the light.

The dogs came in with us.

The year turned its face toward the dark.

We set another log on and waited for Samhain.

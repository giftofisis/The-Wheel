We knew before we saw it.

The light had been changing for weeks — not dramatically, not all at once, but in the particular way it changed at this time of year, arriving a little earlier each morning and staying a little later each evening, the difference too small to measure on any single day and unmistakable across the span of them. The fields were beginning to say something with their colour. The birds had been saying it for a fortnight.

We had been watching the hall.

Not constantly. Not with urgency. Just the way you watch something you know is coming, the way you watch a sky that has weather in it, with the peripheral attention of people who have learned that the important things announce themselves quietly and only to those who are already paying attention.

The morning it happened the air was different before we opened our doors.

Softer. The frost still on the ground but the quality of the cold changed, something taken out of it, the edge gone. The mud at the field's edge had begun to consider moving. In the hedgerow the first green was showing, small and tentative and entirely certain.

We went about our work.

We did not go to the hall.

At the field's edge, where the two paths crossed, we left what we had brought — bread, a cup of something dark, the first green cutting from the hedgerow. We did not linger. We set it down and walked back and did not look behind us, because some things are witnessed by the leaving rather than the watching.

By midmorning the hall fire had changed.

Still burning. Always burning. But different — brighter, the smoke rising straighter, the light it threw across the hillside the particular gold of a thing that has come into its full authority and knows it.

In the lower field the ewes were loud with their lambs.

We did not speak of what had changed in the hall. We had no need to speak of it. We had felt the shift in the air before we opened our doors, had seen it in the hedgerow and the mud and the way the dogs were carrying themselves, and that was enough, that was the whole of it.

The mantle had moved.

The year had stepped into its green half.

We went back to our work and the work was lighter than it had been yesterday, though nothing about it had changed.

We kept the fires low on the longest night.

Not from neglect. Every hearth had been tended since dark fell, the wood stacked, the flames kept. But low was right for the longest night — the light held close rather than thrown outward, the village folding in on itself the way the year folded in on itself, drawing everything toward the centre.

The animals were quiet in their byres. The dogs did not bark.

Snow had been threatening since noon and had not come. The sky held its breath. The stars were unusually bright above the frost-stiff fields, the cold of them a presence rather than an absence, something with weight.

On the far hill, the hall fire burned.

Not low. Steady and upright and visible from every corner of the village, the one fire that did not fold inward on this night, the one light held outward. We did not look at it directly. We had all lived here long enough to know that the hall fire on the longest night was not for looking at directly. It was for knowing was there. It was for the particular quality of warmth that comes not from a fire you can see but from a fire you know is burning.

Toward midnight the wind dropped completely.

We held our breath alongside the sky.

In the stone building on the hill's other side — not the hall, the lower place, the one with the single window that glowed orange through the dark — something was happening that had happened every year since before the oldest among us could remember, and would happen every year after the youngest had gone to their rest.

The light in the window changed quality just before dawn.

Those of us who were still awake felt it before we saw it. A shift in the dark. Something released. Something taken up.

By the time the first grey light came over the eastern fields the dogs were moving again, restless and glad, and the cattle in their byres had turned to face the doors.

The year's new fire was lit from the old.

We exhaled.

We did not sleep the short night.

Not from anxiety — there was nothing anxious about it, there never had been. We stayed awake because the short night asked to be witnessed, the way the long night asked to be held. Different vigils for different hinges of the year. At Yule we folded inward and kept our fires low and waited. At Litha we went out.

The fires on the hill had been burning since before dark.

Not the hall fire — the open fires, the ones we built ourselves on the high ground above the village where you could see in every direction, where the sky was biggest, where the light at midsummer barely left before it began returning. We had carried the wood up in the afternoon, the long golden afternoon that went on and on, the sun refusing to drop, the air warm and loud with insects and the smell of the hay cut that week lying in the fields below.

We sat with the fires through the brief dark.

The hill was loud with us — voices and movement and the particular looseness of people awake at an hour the body doesn't expect wakefulness, a little giddy with the heat and the light and the knowledge of what the night was. Children who should have been sleeping were not sleeping and we let them stay because this was the night for staying.

On the far side of the hill, away from our fires, the other fire burned.

We felt it more than saw it. A brightness at the hill's edge that had a different quality to our own fires — older, steadier, the kind of light that knows what it is for. We did not go to it. We kept our own fires and let that one keep itself.

Just before the sky began to lighten in the northeast the hill went quiet.

Not silent — the insects were still loud, the fire still crackling, a child still awake somewhere at the hill's edge. But quiet underneath all of that. A held quality. The hinge of the year turning on its point, the light at its peak, the dark at its furthest and beginning its return.

We felt it the way we always felt it.

Not loss. Not yet. Just the knowledge of what the fullness contained.

The sky began to lighten.

We fed the fires one last time and watched the sun come up over the eastern fields, the same sun that had barely gone, the long gold of it lying across the hay and the rooftops and the faces of the people on the hill who had stayed awake to see it.

We came down off the hill with the sunrise at our backs.

The village smelled of woodsmoke and cut hay and the particular sweetness of a night spent outside in midsummer.

We went to our beds as the light was already climbing.

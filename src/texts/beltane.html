The hawthorn was out.

Not the suggestion of it, not the first tentative white along the hedgerow, but fully, completely, recklessly out — every branch of the old tree at the garden's edge loaded with blossom until it bowed slightly under its own extravagance, and the smell of it moving across the grass in warm pulses with every shift of the May evening air. Sweet and green and faintly animal. The smell that doesn't have a polite name.

The fire was already laid but not yet lit, a careful construction of seasoned oak in the centre of the lawn, patient and waiting. Around it the grass had been pressed flat by feet arriving all afternoon, and at the far end of the garden the maypole stood with its ribbons hanging loose in the still air — red and white and green and gold, catching the last of the five o'clock sun.

Somewhere in the apple tree, a blackbird was being completely unreasonable about it all.

The table was full. Bread and cheese and early strawberries and things in bowls covered with cloth against the bees, and at the centre of it the sun-plate, waiting. Bottles stood at the table's edge without ceremony — wine dark and wine pale, and a jug of lemonade sweating quietly in the warmth, a sprig of mint laid across its mouth.

At the garden's centre, older than anyone's memory of the garden, a stone sundial stood in the grass with moss in its lettering, patient and permanent.

The light was that particular gold that only happens in late April, low and warm and slightly disbelieving of itself, the kind of light that makes everything it touches look like it's been keeping a secret all winter and has finally decided to tell it.

— — —

Harry lit it without announcement.

The dancing was still unwinding, the guitar still finding its way through something slow and evening-shaped, and he simply walked to the fire, crouched down at the base of the oak construction, and struck a match. He held it to the kindling with both hands cupped around the small flame the way you cup your hands around something you don't want the wind to take.

It caught on the second try.

He stayed crouched for a moment, watching the flame move from kindling to the first thin branches, making sure. Then he stood, put his hands in his pockets, and stepped back.

That was all.

Lara stood at Harry's shoulder and watched the fire take.

"There he is," she said quietly.

Harry nodded once.

— — —

It started with Morgan sitting down.

Not at the fire's edge exactly, but close enough to feel the heat on one side and the cool May dark on the other. Someone had brought a low wooden stool from the kitchen and set it near the flames without being asked, as though the garden had anticipated the need. Morgan sat, and settled, and the guitar found a drone — a single open chord, low and warm, held without progression. Just a sound. Just the underneath of things.

David's hands found the tabletop again, softer this time. A heartbeat. Steady and unhurried.

The garden didn't go silent so much as it leaned in.

There was a man who walked into the woods in May.

He didn't know why. He'd told himself he was going for the air, for the exercise, for no particular reason at all. But his feet knew the way without being shown, and the path he followed was not the one he'd taken before, and the wood that closed around him was greener and louder and more alive than any wood he could remember, as though the year had been saving itself for this particular evening and had decided, without consulting him, that he was the reason.

The birds were loud with it. The bees were loud with it. The whole wood was loud with something that had no name except yes.

Morgan paused. The drum held the heartbeat. The guitar held the drone.

"Yes," Morgan said quietly.

The fire crackled once, sharp and clean, as though it agreed.

— — —

And in the garden, the fire burned down to its last coal, a single point of deep red light in the May dark, steady and unhurried, burning long after it had any reason to.

The hawthorn gave off its sweetness to no one and everyone.

The sundial held the dark patiently, waiting for the sun it knew was coming.

The wheel had turned.

The year was open.

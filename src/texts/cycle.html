The Cycle

The tent and blankets and food eased your body, The woodsmoke and sounds eased your mind, You fell asleep, The Pounding of hooves, Beating of drums, Woke you, Soft in the wind, Sharp in the chest, Far in the night sky, Near in the marrow, You moved, out of the circle of ancestors, Dancing to the beating of drums, Ahead, a figure cloaked you saw, You Called to us, As I breathed deeply beneath the earth, You Called to us “May I ride with the rade until the break of day?”. My son, the sage, the horned hunter, “Catch my hand and dare not let go!” They rode through and upon me, Through valleys and hills, Past coasts of soft and gentle blue, Through rivers of whirling white, Through bustling towns, and sleepy villages. The dawning broke, My son spoke, “The Dawning comes and thou art back again from whence thou sprang upon my steed. Mine bargain is kept, for thou rode with the rade until the break of day” “Let me tarry awhile and see the great spiral. Let me go with thee now into thy dark bower. Outside, His father stood watching from the snows, Holly in his prime, who would father no son, For he was the one I had borne before, The one I would bear when his reign was through. One sovereignty, two faces. One king, two names. This is the cycle. This is the cost. I have been mother to every Oak king born, Since the turning of the first cold. I will mourn this one when the harvest comes, As I have mourned each one. As I will mourn the one who watches now, From the snows outside. Snow lay still on the high fields that morning, The wind came down off the moor with teeth bared, Out walked Oak, among his ewes in the cold, The lamb of the season warmed within his embrace. He counted them then. The stock and the staying. The broken fences. The breath in the air. The work of late winter, Of what the land asked, Not yet his reign, But readiness for it. He looked toward the hall on the far hill rising, Where Holly sat watching the smoke from his fires, He knew what was held in that watching, And held it back, For the time was not yet. Though the time was near. Holly stood at the Hall’s window in silence. He had been the one in the field, two turnings before. He had counted the stock and held the new lambs. He knew what Oak knew. Oak knew that he knew. That knowing had passed between them across the snows, As it had passed between every Oak and Holly since the turning of the first cold. This is the cycle. This is the cost. The young king labouring in his mother's weather, The old king watching from the seat of his power, Each one seeing the other and seeing himself, Each one bound to what was coming, Each one willing. The hawthorn was heavy with white that morning, The green of the year had come in fully, We walked out together into the woods, Not as god and goddess, But as the young always walk. He took my hand at the edge of the glade. I was no older than the green around us. The leaves were new. The air was loud with bees. The earth beneath our feet was warm for the first time. There he kissed me, There in the dappled light, Back I kissed him, The wood drew close around us, The deer at the edge, The hare in the grass, The fox with her kits at the mouth of her den, The birds in the high branches, gone suddenly still. We lay down together in the bluebells. Oak in his prime, The green king, The year's full sovereign, I in my maiden-aspect, The year's young mother, The act was simple, The act was old, The act was what the season had been turning toward. This is the cycle. This is the cost. At the moment he gave himself into me and I received him, The new king was seeded, The wood erupted into sound around us, every bird in the canopy lifting its voice at once, The bees roaring, The hare drumming the earth, The deer calling across the spring valleys, The fox-kits crying out from the den's dark mouth, The green itself singing with the rising sap, The whole wood was loud with the world saying, Yes, To the king already growing inside his mother. Oak knew, in that moment, what he had done. He had fathered his own ending. Knowingly. Willingly. As the cycle requires it, He is the cycle, The green that he was had to give way at the harvest, So the dark king who would be born at midsummer Could one day take up what he himself had set down. He held me in the bluebells as the sound went on, The new king turned once in the dark of my body, The year continued its turning toward summer. The pain I'd borne a thousand times before Came on me again in the year's highest light, The day long and golden, Held at the hinge, The light at its peak, and the fire burned bright, upon the open hill where I laboured alone. The corn stood gold on the low fields that morning, The heat came down off the high blue sky in waves, Holly walked out among his sheaves in the sun, The scythe of the season, Warmed within his hands. He cut them then. The wheat and the waiting. The barley laid down. The dust in the light. The work of high summer, What the land gave to him, Not yet his reign, The fullness before it. The apples hung heavy on the orchard's old branches. The press stood ready in the yard for the cider. The bread of the first sheaf was rising in the kitchen, The smell of it travelled across the warm fields. He looked toward the hall on the far hill standing, Where Oak sat watching the dust of the threshing, He knew what was held in that watching, Held it back, For the time was not yet, Though the time was near. Oak stood at the Hall’s window in silence. He had been the one in the field, two turnings before. He had cut the wheat and trodden the apples. He knew what Holly knew, Holly knew that he knew, The knowing passed between them across the golden fields, As it has passed between every Holly and every Oak, Since the turning of the first warm. The first loaf came from the oven. The cider began to run. The scythe rang once on a stone in the open field. Oak turned from the window and tasted the new bread, Holly bent again to the cutting, And did not look up. The light was shortening. They both could feel it. The mantle was waiting. Neither one moved it. Yet. He walked out into the gold of the morning, His crown of summer laid down with the grain, Holly came forward, No older than spring, And took what his other-self set on the ground. There was no battle. There was no fight. There never had been. One face of the king gave way to the next, As the year gave way to itself in turning, As the leaf gives way to the branch that bore it. This is the cycle. This is the cost. And the blood went down through root and stone to where I waited, I received him, As I have received every Oak king before, His face in the dark of the earth was my face, His rest in the dark of the earth was my rest, The new king walking the cooling fields, Wore the same sovereignty, Under a different name. The bare branches were dark against the cold sky that evening, The last leaves had fallen, The year had thinned, We walked out together through the town, Not as god and goddess, But as the old always walk, Our faces hidden under the masks of the season. I was the crone, My hair white beneath the hood. He was the sage, His beard grey at the chin. We carried lanterns. We knew the streets by heart. We did the work of the night as the townsfolk did, Guided a spirit through the lych-gate to its rest, Left bread on a doorstep where a name was being spoken, Turned a cold-eyed thing back from a child's window, Walked the boundary stones and renewed the salt. The mortal dead were close. The unkind dead were closer. We did what was needed. No one knew us by our names. We were two old people walking the streets at Samhain, That was the truth of it, As much as any other truth, Because the cycle wears whatever face the season needs. When the work was done, We came at last to the cottage, The same low cottage where the kings have always lived, When they grew between birth and reign, rested between reigns, The cauldron hung black over the fire on the hearth, The table was spread with the fruits of the dying year: Pomegranate split open, its seeds like dark jewels, Apples cut in halves to show the stars at their cores, Late plums, Dried figs, Walnuts in their shells, Bread torn, not cut, A jug of dark wine. We ate together in the firelight. We did not speak much. Holly in his prime, The dark king, The year's full sovereign, I in my crone-aspect, The year's old mother, We knew each other across the table without words, Because we had done this before, and would, again. This is the cycle. This is the cost. When we lay down together on the cottage floor by the cauldron By the fire, By the seeded fruit, The act was simple, The act was old, The act was what the season had been turning toward. At the moment he gave himself into me I received him, The new king was seeded, Every sound in the world stopped at once. The fire did not crackle. The wind did not move. The dead in the streets outside fell silent. The owl in the bare tree did not call. The cauldron did not bubble. The dark held its breath. Even my own heart paused in my chest, His with it, The whole world was still for the long held moment, The new king taking root in the dark of the year, In the dark of my body, Where light begins its impossible return. Holly knew, In that moment, What he had done. He had fathered his own ending. Knowingly. Willingly, Because the cycle requires it, He is the cycle, The dark that he was had to give way to the spring equinox So the green king who would be born at midwinter Could one day take up what he himself had set down. The silence broke slowly, The way frost breaks. The fire crackled once. The owl called in the distance. The world began to move again, Differently, Because the seed had taken, The year would turn, The light was already, invisibly, returning. The cycle was told and the seed set. You have heard me speak through every season, through every birth, through every death, through the dark and the light of the year, and you know me now, in all my faces. You called to us, "I know who you are! You are the mother of all, and I do not fear you!" I called to you, "Only those who do not know me, fear me." The Crone fell from my face like a shawl let drop, and I stood before you as I had always stood, ancient and unchanged, and you saw me whole. My son, the sage, the horned hunter, waited. He had not gone. He never goes. He carries each soul in and each soul out, and the bargain we struck must be honoured both ways. You mounted up. The rade rode again, but downward this time, through the deep places, through the realms I hold. We passed spirits at dark pools, resting in alder shadow, the long grey moss soft beneath their hands. We passed children playing in sunlit meadows, the bright ones, the shining people who waved as we passed. We passed beings of glorious oak, beings of robust holly, beings of exquisite light, beings of exquisite dark, the thousand kings I have borne and mourned and held. At a boundary, the hunter rumbled, "Here goes one who has rested and now desires to resume the great dance of life." A figure clothed in grey moved toward an opening, a void with a single spot of light impossibly far away, impossibly close. The rade rose then. Through dark earth, Past pebbles and snails and worms and creatures of the deep earth, through root and stone and the very substance of me, back toward the surface, back toward the dawn. You woke at the fire you had left. The tent. The blankets. The ancestors gone. The smell of woodsmoke still on the air. But you had passed through me twice, You carried the cycle in you now, As I carry every soul who has ridden with the rade returned, knowing. I am here. Always.
